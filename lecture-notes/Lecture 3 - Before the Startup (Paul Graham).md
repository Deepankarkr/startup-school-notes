# **Lecture 3: Before the Startup (Paul Graham)** 

---

## **Core Idea**

Startups are **highly counterintuitive**. Your instincts will often be wrong → you must **override them consciously**.

---

## **1. Trust vs Not Trust Instincts**

* Don’t trust instincts about **startups** (they mislead).
* Do trust instincts about **people**.
* Work with people you **like, respect, and know well**.

---

## **2. What You Actually Need**

* Not expertise in startups.
* You need **deep understanding of users**.
* Success = knowing users well (example: Facebook).

---

## **3. “Playing Startup” Problem**

Many founders:

* Raise money
* Hire people
* Rent office

But fail because they ignore the only thing that matters:
→ **make something people want**

Reason: people are trained to **game systems** (school, exams).

---

## **4. No “Tricks” in Startups**

* No shortcuts, no “growth hacks”
* Real growth = **users love product + tell others**
* Gaming works in school/jobs, **not in startups**

Users only care:
→ does product solve their problem?

---

## **5. Startups Are All-Consuming**

* Takes over your life (years/decade)
* Problems never reduce, only change
* High responsibility, constant pressure

---

## **6. Don’t Start in College**

* Startup = full-time commitment
* You can’t be both student + founder

Better use college for:

* Exploration
* Learning
* Trying different things

Starting early removes:

* Freedom
* Serendipity
* Life options

Also: you’re **more likely to succeed later**.

---

## **7. Can You Succeed?**

* You **can’t predict** if you’ll be a good founder
* Even experts can’t judge this well
* Only way to know = **try (later)**

---

## **8. What To Do in College**

You need:

* **Ideas**
* **Co-founders**

Best way to get both:

* Work on interesting problems
* With people you like

---

## **9. Startup Ideas**

Wrong approach:

* Trying to think of startup ideas

Right approach:

* Build things you **personally care about**
* Ideas emerge **naturally (side projects)**

Examples:

* Google, Facebook, Apple → started as side projects

---

## **10. How to Get Good Ideas**

Do 3 things:

1. Learn about **important things**
2. Work on **interesting problems**
3. Work with **people you respect**

---

## **11. What Problems to Work On**

* Hard to define generally
* But:

  * Interesting problems feel **naturally engaging**
  * Often ignored by others initially

Good heuristic:

* If something feels genuinely interesting → pursue it

---

## **12. “Live in the Future”**

* Be at the **edge of technology trends**
* What seems obvious to you now → becomes startups later

Example:

* Early VoIP (built just to talk cheaply)

---

## **13. Learning Strategy**

Best preparation:

* Not startup courses
* Not business school

Instead:
→ **Learn powerful things deeply**

Domain expertise matters most.

---

## **14. Key Advice**

* Follow curiosity
* Work on meaningful problems
* Don’t optimize early for startups

Final principle:
→ **“Just learn”**

---

## **Extra Insights (Q&A)**

* Non-technical founders:

  * Domain expertise or **sales** role

* Business school:

  * Not useful for early startups (focus is management, needed later)

* Hiring early:

  * People should be **self-driven like founders**
s
* Female founders:

  * Strong growth metrics overcome bias

* When side project becomes startup:

  * When it **takes over your life**

* Slow growth:

  * Do things that **don’t scale**

---

## **Final Takeaways**

* Startups = **no tricks, only real value**
* Focus on **users + problems**
* Don’t rush → prepare by **learning + exploring**
* Best ideas come **indirectly, not by force**
